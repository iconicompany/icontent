When a company decides to implement AI, the first mistake is almost never related to choosing a model, framework, or vendor.
It happens earlier - **in choosing the format of collaboration with a specialist**.

The intuitive business response is: "We need to hire an AI expert."
But the correct question is different: **in what format do you need a change architect?**

---

## Why AI Implementations Don't Pay Off

Demand for automation is growing, but so is caution.
The reason is simple: too many cases where:

* pilots have been launched,
* budgets have been spent,
* presentations are ready,

but internal company processes remained chaotic.

In 80% of cases, the problem is not with the model or the code quality.
The problem is the lack of a **systemic architecture for change**.

AI is implemented into processes that:

* are not described,
* are not measured,
* have no owner,
* are not tied to economics.

In such a context, any model turns into an expensive toy.

---

## Who Businesses Truly Need

There are very few specialists on the market who:

1. first analyze "as-is" processes,
2. find bottlenecks with real economic impact,
3. prioritize,
4. calculate payback,
5. and only then launch implementation.

These are not "AI Engineers" or "ML Developers."
These are **AI Strategists and Transformation Architects**.

And an important point: such specialists **almost never work full-time as permanent employees**.
Such are the current market realities.

---

## Three Working Formats

Once it's clear *who* is needed, the main question arises - **in what format to work with them**. In practice, I see three options.

---

### 1. Part-time

The AI strategist works 2-3 days a week and delves quite deeply into the company's context.

**Suitable if:**

* there is already a manageable internal structure,
* there is a strong team capable of implementing solutions,
* processes are at least partially described.

**Plus:** deep involvement and the feeling of "an insider."
**Minus:** strong dependence on the maturity of the internal team and management.

---

### 2. Subscription to an AI Strategist

Work begins with a diagnosis:

* process analysis,
* identification of loss points,
* assessment of economic potential.

Next, a **roadmap** is formed:

* priorities,
* metrics,
* ROI calculation.

Following this, there is regular architectural support for implementation - not a one-time consultation, but systematic work.

**Suitable if:**

* order needs to be established,
* there is no internal expertise,
* predictable payback is crucial.

From an ROI perspective - **the most sustainable and manageable format**.

---

### 3. Full-time (permanent staff)

A classic option that makes sense:

* for a large company scale,
* with a constant flow of tasks,
* with a mature product and management culture.

In practice, strong AI architects rarely choose one project for the long term, so this format doesn't always work as businesses expect.

---

## A Bit About Money - And A Lot About Mistakes

The market is not yet standardized:

* **full-time:** 400,000 - 1,000,000 ₽ per month,
* **subscription:** 100,000 - 300,000 ₽,
* **part-time:** proportional to workload.

But the key question is **not about the rate**.

The most expensive part of an AI project is **the format mistake**.
An incorrect interaction model often costs more than the specialist themselves and all pilots combined.

A professional will work qualitatively in any format.
The difference is in:

* depth of involvement,
* speed of decision-making,
* and **how quickly you achieve payback**.

---

### Instead of a Conclusion

The format of working with an AI strategist is a **strategic decision**, not a staffing issue.
It is logical to first analyze the structure of processes and business goals, and only then choose the interaction model.
