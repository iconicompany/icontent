And we were flying nowhere.

We conducted dialogues with candidates in chats.
Manual screening.
Correspondence.
Calls.

Per day - 3-5 people. Sometimes 7, if we really pushed it.

I look at this and think:
something doesn't add up.

The funnel was huge.
Lots of responses.
But almost nothing at the output.

I ask the team:

- Why so slow?
- It's normal, that's the market
- Everyone works like this
- Candidates are difficult now
- Quality recruitment cannot be fast

And it sounds logical.

You start to believe it.

---

Time passes.

Nothing changes.

The same 5 candidates a day.
The same conversations.
The same explanations.

And at some point, you catch yourself thinking:

maybe this really is the maximum?

---

But no.

It's not the market.
It's not the candidates.
It's not "niche specifics".

It's simply a system that doesn't scale.

---

## The Most Unpleasant Part Isn't Slow Work

The most unpleasant thing is when a team normalizes a problem.

- But we communicate with quality
- We're not about volume, we're about depth
- Better less, but better

And this is already dangerous.

Because behind beautiful words hides a simple truth:

**we can't handle the volume**

---

## The Tipping Point

It was simple.

We asked ourselves a question:

> If tomorrow the number of applications increased tenfold, what would change?

Answer: nothing.
We would simply drown.

And it became obvious:

the problem wasn't with the people.
the problem was with the process.

---

## What We Did

We stopped believing in "that's how it's done".
We started counting:

- how many candidates per day
- how many reached the interview stage
- how many progressed further
- where they dropped off

And suddenly it turned out that "quality recruitment" = simply a bottleneck.

---

Secondly, we eliminated manual hell.

❌ Dialogues → ✅ automation
❌ Screening → ✅ system
❌ Subjective assessment → ✅ algorithm

Because if a process cannot be scaled, it's not a process; it's manual labor.

---

## And Most Importantly

We stopped justifying slow work with quality.

Because in 90% of cases, it's not about quality.

It's about the lack of a system.

---

Now I look at those 5 candidates a day and realize:

we weren't "doing quality work".

we simply couldn't do more.
