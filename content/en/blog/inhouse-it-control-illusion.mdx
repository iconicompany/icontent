"In-house IT is more reliable than an external team."

I hear this every time at meetings.

And every time it sounds like confidence.

But in reality - it's often an illusion of control.

---

## The truth looks different

In-house IT almost always turns into a system where:

- everything relies on 2-3 key people
- knowledge lives in chats and heads
- documentation "will happen someday"
- changes are feared to be touched

From the outside, it looks like stability.
Inside - it's a dependence on people.

---

## And here's what happens next

One vacation.
One departure.
One overloaded engineer.

And suddenly:

- no one understands how it works
- any changes become a risk
- the business starts "waiting for the person"

This is not control.
This is manual mode.

---

## "But their own people react faster"

Yes. While they are in place.

The problem is elsewhere:

- there is speed, but no system
- there is knowledge, but it doesn't scale
- there are solutions, but they are not reproducible

This is not IT architecture.
This is operational dependency.

---

## A project team looks "external"

Until you start counting.

A normal project team has:

- knowledge not in heads, but in the system
- there is interchangeability
- there are artifacts, not "people's memory"
- there is responsibility for the result, not for the process

---

## The most unpleasant part

In-house seems cheaper and more reliable.

Until you start paying for:

- downtime
- errors
- "fire-fighting mode" accelerations
- and dependence on one person

---

## The reality is simple

In-house IT gives a feeling of control.
A project-based model gives control.

These are different things.

---

The question is not whether you have your own IT.

The question is different:

> Can your system work without the people who "know everything"?

If not - you don't have a team.
You have a risk.
